EMTH516 – Unit 1: Review of Groups

Learning Outcomes​

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

  • Understand the concept of binary operations on sets.
  • Analyze algebraic structures such as groups and subgroups.
  • Understand the properties of cosets and normal subgroups.
  • State and prove Lagrange’s theorem.
  • Find the order of an element of a group.
  • Define cyclic and quotient (factor) groups.
  • Define and work with homomorphisms and isomorphisms between groups.

1. Introduction​

Group theory is one of the oldest and most powerful branches of abstract algebra. It finds applications across mathematics, physics, chemistry, and computer science. Its origins lie in the work of Lagrange, Ruffini, and Galois during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

This unit provides a comprehensive review of groups, subgroups, normal subgroups, cosets, homomorphisms, isomorphisms, and related results that serve as foundations for later topics such as rings, fields, and Galois theory.

1.1 Definition of Group and Subgroup​

Binary Operations on a Set​

A binary operation ∗*∗ on a non-empty set SSS is a function ∗:S×S→S* : S \times S \to S∗:S×S→S that assigns every ordered pair (a,b)∈S×S(a, b) \in S \times S(a,b)∈S×S to a unique element a∗b∈Sa * b \in Sa∗b∈S.

Examples​

  1. (Integers under addition): Addition, subtraction, and multiplication of integers are binary operations on Z\mathbb{Z}Z.
  2. (Function composition): If XXX is a non-empty set and F(X)F(X)F(X) denotes all functions from X→XX \to XX→X, then function composition is a binary operation on F(X)F(X)F(X).

Non-examples​

  1. Subtraction is not a binary operation on N\mathbb{N}N (e.g. 2−3=−1∉N2 - 3 = -1 \notin \mathbb{N}2−3=−1∈/N).
  2. Division is not a binary operation on Z\mathbb{Z}Z (e.g. 1/2∉Z1 / 2 \notin \mathbb{Z}1/2∈/Z).

Properties of Binary Operations​

Let ∗*∗ be a binary operation on SSS.

  1. Closure: a∗b∈S, ∀a,b∈S.a * b \in S,\ \forall a,b \in S.a∗b∈S, ∀a,b∈S.
  2. Associativity: (a∗b)∗c=a∗(b∗c).(a * b) * c = a * (b * c).(a∗b)∗c=a∗(b∗c).
  3. Identity Element: An element e∈Se \in Se∈S such that e∗a=a∗e=a, ∀a∈S.e * a = a * e = a,\ \forall a \in S.e∗a=a∗e=a, ∀a∈S.
  4. Inverse Element: For a∈Sa \in Sa∈S, an element b∈Sb \in Sb∈S such that a∗b=b∗a=e.a * b = b * a = e.a∗b=b∗a=e.
  5. Commutativity: a∗b=b∗a, ∀a,b∈S.a * b = b * a,\ \forall a,b \in S.a∗b=b∗a, ∀a,b∈S.

Examples of Properties​

  • Addition on Z\mathbb{Z}Z is associative and commutative, with identity 000 and inverses −a-a−a.
  • Multiplication on Z\mathbb{Z}Z is associative and commutative but not every element has an inverse (only 1 and -1).
  • Subtraction on Z\mathbb{Z}Z is neither associative nor commutative and lacks an identity.

Algebraic Structures​

  • Quasi-group: Any non-empty set with a binary operation.
  • Semigroup: A set with an associative binary operation.
  • Monoid: A semigroup with an identity element.
  • Group: A monoid in which every element has an inverse.

Definition 1.1.15 (Group)​

A group is a non-empty set GGG equipped with a binary operation ∗*∗ satisfying:

  1. Associativity: (a∗b)∗c=a∗(b∗c), ∀a,b,c∈G.(a*b)*c = a*(b*c),\ \forall a,b,c \in G.(a∗b)∗c=a∗(b∗c), ∀a,b,c∈G.
  2. Identity: There exists e∈Ge \in Ge∈G such that e∗a=a∗e=a, ∀a∈G.e*a = a*e = a,\ \forall a \in G.e∗a=a∗e=a, ∀a∈G.
  3. Inverse: For each a∈Ga \in Ga∈G, there exists a−1∈Ga^{-1} \in Ga−1∈G such that a∗a−1=a−1∗a=e.a*a^{-1} = a^{-1}*a = e.a∗a−1=a−1∗a=e.

A group is abelian if it also satisfies commutativity.

Examples​

  1. (Z,+)(\mathbb{Z}, +)(Z,+) – Group under addition.
  2. (Q∗,×)(\mathbb{Q}^*, \times)(Q∗,×) – Non-zero rationals under multiplication.
  3. (M2(R),+)(M_{2}(\mathbb{R}), +)(M2​(R),+) – 2×2 real matrices under addition.
  4. {1,−1,i,−i}\{1, -1, i, -i\}{1,−1,i,−i} under complex multiplication – finite group of order 4.

Subgroups​

A non-empty subset H⊆GH \subseteq GH⊆G is a subgroup of GGG if it is itself a group under the same operation.

Theorem 1.1.27 (Subgroup Criterion)​

A non-empty subset H⊆GH \subseteq GH⊆G ius a subgroup of GGG iff ab−1∈H, ∀a,b∈H.ab^{-1} \in H,\ \forall a,b \in H.ab−1∈H, ∀a,b∈H.

Example​

Let G=(R,+)G = (\mathbb{R}, +)G=(R,+) and H=(Z,+)H = (\mathbb{Z}, +)H=(Z,+). Then HHH is a subgroup of GGG.

1.2 Normal Subgroups and Cosets​

Cosets​

If HHH is a subgroup of GGG and g∈Gg \in Gg∈G, the left coset of HHH in GGG is gH={gh∣h∈H}gH = \{gh \mid h \in H\}gH={gh∣h∈H}, and the right coset is Hg={hg∣h∈H}.Hg = \{hg \mid h \in H\}.Hg={hg∣h∈H}.

Cosets partition the group into disjoint subsets of equal size.

Theorem 1.2.5 (Lagrange’s Theorem)​

If HHH is a subgroup of finite group GGG, then the order of HHH divides the order of GGG:

∣G∣=[G:H]⋅∣H∣|G| = [G:H] \cdot |H|∣G∣=[G:H]⋅∣H∣

Normal Subgroups​

A subgroup H⊴GH \trianglelefteq GH⊴G (read “H is normal in G”) if gH=Hg, ∀g∈G.gH = Hg,\ \forall g \in G.gH=Hg, ∀g∈G.

Equivalent condition: g−1hg∈H, ∀g∈G,h∈H.g^{-1}hg \in H,\ \forall g \in G, h \in H.g−1hg∈H, ∀g∈G,h∈H.

Example​

The center of a group Z(G)={x∈G∣xg=gx, ∀g∈G}Z(G) = \{x \in G \mid xg = gx,\ \forall g \in G\}Z(G)={x∈G∣xg=gx, ∀g∈G} is a normal subgroup.

1.3 Order of Elements and Factor Groups​

Order of an Element​

The order of an element a∈Ga \in Ga∈G is the least positive integer nnn such that an=ea^n = ean=e. If no such nnn exists, aaa is of infinite order.

Example​

In (C∗,×)(\mathbb{C}^*, \times)(C∗,×), iii has order 4 since i4=1.i^4 = 1.i4=1.

Cyclic Groups​

A group GGG is cyclic if there exists an element a∈Ga \in Ga∈G such that every element of GGG can be written as a power of aaa. We write G=⟨a⟩.G = \langle a \rangle.G=⟨a⟩.

Every cyclic group is abelian.

Factor (Quotient) Groups​

If H⊴GH \trianglelefteq GH⊴G, the set of all right cosets G/H={Hg∣g∈G}G/H = \{Hg \mid g \in G\}G/H={Hg∣g∈G} forms a group under the operation:

(Ha)(Hb)=H(ab)(Ha)(Hb) = H(ab)(Ha)(Hb)=H(ab)

This group is called the quotient group of GGG modulo HHH.

1.4 Group Homomorphisms​

A function ϕ:(G,∗)→(G′,∘)\phi: (G, *) \to (G', \circ)ϕ:(G,∗)→(G′,∘) is a group homomorphism if

ϕ(a∗b)=ϕ(a)∘ϕ(b), ∀a,b∈G.\phi(a*b) = \phi(a) \circ \phi(b),\ \forall a,b \in G.ϕ(a∗b)=ϕ(a)∘ϕ(b), ∀a,b∈G.

Key Results​

  • ϕ(eG)=eG′\phi(e_G) = e_{G'}ϕ(eG​)=eG′​
  • ϕ(a−1)=(ϕ(a))−1\phi(a^{-1}) = (\phi(a))^{-1}ϕ(a−1)=(ϕ(a))−1

Kernel and Image​

  • Kernel: ker⁡(ϕ)={g∈G∣ϕ(g)=e′}\ker(\phi) = \{g \in G \mid \phi(g) = e'\}ker(ϕ)={g∈G∣ϕ(g)=e′}
  • Image: Im(ϕ)={ϕ(g)∣g∈G}\text{Im}(\phi) = \{\phi(g) \mid g \in G\}Im(ϕ)={ϕ(g)∣g∈G}

Theorem​

The kernel of a homomorphism is a normal subgroup of GGG. The image is a subgroup of G′G'G′.

Fundamental Theorem of Homomorphisms​

If ϕ:G→G′\phi: G \to G'ϕ:G→G′ is a homomorphism, then

G/ker⁡(ϕ)≅Im(ϕ).G/\ker(\phi) \cong \text{Im}(\phi).G/ker(ϕ)≅Im(ϕ).

1.5 Permutation Groups and Integers Modulo n​

Symmetric Group​

The symmetric group on nnn symbols, denoted SnS_nSn​, is the set of all bijections from {1,2,…,n}\{1,2,\dots,n\}{1,2,…,n} to itself under composition.
∣Sn∣=n!|S_n| = n!∣Sn​∣=n!

Each element of SnS_nSn​ is a permutation and can be written in cycle notation.

Alternating Group​

The alternating group AnA_nAn​ is the subgroup of even permutations of SnS_nSn​, with order n!/2.n!/2.n!/2.

Group of Integers Modulo n​

Let Zn={0,1,2,…,n−1}\mathbb{Z}_n = \{0,1,2,\dots,n-1\}Zn​={0,1,2,…,n−1} with operation addition modulo nnn. Then (Zn,+n)(\mathbb{Z}_n, +_n)(Zn​,+n​) is a finite cyclic group of order nnn.

Summary​

  • Binary operations and algebraic structures build up to the definition of a group.
  • Subgroups, cosets, and normal subgroups structure groups into layers.
  • Lagrange’s theorem links subgroup size to the group order.
  • Homomorphisms preserve group structure, with kernels leading to quotient groups.
  • Symmetric and cyclic groups provide concrete examples of abstract theory.

Glossary​

  • Binary Operation: Rule combining two elements to produce one.
  • Subgroup: Non-empty subset closed under operation and inverses.
  • Normal Subgroup: Subgroup invariant under conjugation.
  • Cyclic Group: Generated by one element.
  • Homomorphism: Structure-preserving map between groups.
  • Isomorphism: Bijective homomorphism.
