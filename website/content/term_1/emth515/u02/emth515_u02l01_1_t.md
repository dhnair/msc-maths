# U02L01 - Basic Properties of RS Sums (Transcript)

## Learning Outcomes
- Understand the first four basic properties of the Riemann-Stieltjes integral.
- Explain how constants, sums, squares, and products interact with Riemann-Stieltjes integrability.
- Follow detailed proofs based strictly on upper and lower Riemann-Stieltjes sums.

## Introduction
This lecture begins Unit 2 and focuses on core algebraic and structural properties of the Riemann-Stieltjes integral: scalar multiplication, additivity, squaring, and products.

## Definitions
- $f \in R(\alpha)$ on $[a,b]$ means upper and lower RS integrals coincide.
- $U(P,f,\alpha)$ and $L(P,f,\alpha)$ denote upper and lower RS sums.
- $M_i$ and $m_i$ are supremum and infimum of $f$ on $[x_{i-1},x_i]$.

## Main Development

### 1. Property: $cf \in R(\alpha)$ and $\int_a^b c f d\alpha = c \int_a^b f d\alpha$
Case analysis:

#### Case $c=0$
- $cf = 0$, a constant function, hence integrable.
- Integral equals zero, consistent with the result.

#### Case $c>0$
- $M_i(cf) = c M_i(f)$ and $m_i(cf) = c m_i(f)$.
- Compute:
  $$
  U(P,cf,\alpha) - L(P,cf,\alpha)
  = c [ U(P,f,\alpha) - L(P,f,\alpha) ].
  $$
- Since $f \in R(\alpha)$, RHS $< c\epsilon$, giving integrability of $cf$.
- Lower integral:
  $$
  \int_a^b cf d\alpha
  = c \int_a^b f d\alpha.
  $$

#### Case $c<0$
- Bounds reverse: $M_i(cf) = c m_i(f)$ and $m_i(cf) = c M_i(f)$.
- Computation yields:
  $$
  U(P,cf,\alpha) - L(P,cf,\alpha)
  = -c [ U(P,f,\alpha) - L(P,f,\alpha) ].
  $$
- Again integrability follows.
- Final equality same:
  $$
  \int_a^b cf d\alpha
  = c \int_a^b f d\alpha.
  $$

### 2. Property: $(f_1+f_2) \in R(\alpha)$ and additivity of the integral
Given $f_1,f_2 \in R(\alpha)$:

- Choose partitions $P_1,P_2$ with:
  $$
  U(P_1,f_1,\alpha) - L(P_1,f_1,\alpha) < \epsilon/2, \
  U(P_2,f_2,\alpha) - L(P_2,f_2,\alpha) < \epsilon/2.
  $$
- Let $P=P_1 \cup P_2$.
- Using inequalities for infimum and supremum of sums:
  $$
  m_i \ge m_i' + m_i'', \quad M_i \le M_i' + M_i''.
  $$
- Derive:
  $$
  U(P,f,\alpha) - L(P,f,\alpha)
  < \epsilon.
  $$
- Hence $f_1+f_2 \in R(\alpha)$.

To prove additivity:
- Use bounds:
  $$
  \int_a^b f d\alpha \le U(P,f,\alpha) < L(P,f_1,\alpha)+L(P,f_2,\alpha)+\epsilon.
  $$
- Apply corresponding inequality for $-f$.
- Combine to conclude:
  $$
  \int_a^b (f_1+f_2) d\alpha
  = \int_a^b f_1 d\alpha + \int_a^b f_2 d\alpha.
  $$

### 3. Property: If $f \in R(\alpha)$, then $f^2 \in R(\alpha)$
- Since $f$ is bounded, $|f(x)| \le k$.
- For $t_1,t_2$ in subinterval:
  $$
  |f^2(t_1) - f^2(t_2)| \le 2k |f(t_1)-f(t_2)|.
  $$
- Leads to:
  $$
  M_i' - m_i' \le 2k (M_i - m_i).
  $$
- Summing:
  $$
  U(P,f^2,\alpha) - L(P,f^2,\alpha)
  < \epsilon.
  $$
- Hence $f^2 \in R(\alpha)$.

### 4. Property: If $f,g \in R(\alpha)$, then $fg \in R(\alpha)$
- From properties above:
  - $f+g \in R(\alpha)$,
  - $f-g \in R(\alpha)$,
  - $(f+g)^2, (f-g)^2 \in R(\alpha)$.
- Compute:
  $$
  (f+g)^2 - (f-g)^2 = 4fg.
  $$
- Hence $4fg \in R(\alpha)$, and scalar multiplication gives $fg \in R(\alpha)$.

## Summary
This lecture established four foundational algebraic properties of the Riemann-Stieltjes integral:
- Scalar multiplication
- Additivity
- Squaring
- Product of integrable functions
All proofs rely strictly on controlling upper and lower RS sums.
